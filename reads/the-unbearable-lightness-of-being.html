Gentle Reminder: This is not a review. Also, in these unprecedented times don't venture out of your home. Take a book and get lost in the world the writer has created for you only to find yourself in a different colour. Remember, for the time being, a person who longs to leave the place where he lives is an unhappy person.
There is a kind of goodness that's too mystical to capture in words, therefore a book like The Unbearable Lightness of Being is too unbearable to be reviewed; heart speaks only to praise, only to recapture, only to summarize. And when the heart speaks, the mind finds it indecent to object. A normal book will answer a question, but a good book will ask you a question and then show you all possible answers without attempting to persuade you into choosing anyone. Because the good writer knows that there is no correct choice, and the writer also knows: 'Es muss sein' ("It must be", in German); choices are not free will but rather an inescapable fate. What we have not chosen we cannot consider either our merit or our failure. "To rebel against being born a woman seemed as foolish to her as to take pride in it". Or to state it conversely, we all get to choose our sufferings but suffer we must.
The premise of this book is veiled behind the question, what shall we choose? Weight or lightness? Through Tomas — a womanizer who lives for his work, his wife Tereza, his mistress Sabina, Sabina's lover Franz, and Karenin — the dog of Tomas and Tereza, the writer investigates what it means to choose either of weight or lightness. And why shall humans be reduced to imagination and fiction to learn the answer to binary questions like that? Why should one read a novel to answer such a simple but hard question? The writer answers: There is no means to test which decision is better, because there is no basis for comparison. And what can life be worth if the first rehearsal for life is life itself? Any schoolboy can do experiments in the physics laboratory to test various scientific hypotheses. But man, because he has only one life to live, cannot conduct experiments to test whether to follow his passion or not.
If we choose the weight, then the writer argues that the heaviness of the burden can crush us by pinning us to the ground. We sink beneath the burden. And yet the heaviest of burden is simultaneously an image of life's most intense fulfilment. The heavier the burden, the closer our lives come to the earth, the more real and truthful they become. Conversely, the absolute absence of a burden causes man to be lighter than air, to soar into heights, take leave of the earth and his earthly beings, and become only half real, his movements as free as they are insignificant. We believe that the greatness of man sterns from the fact that he bears his fate as Atlas bore the heavens on his shoulders.
If we choose lightness, then the writer argues that history is as light as individual human life, unbearable light, light as feather, as dust swirling into the air, as whatever will no longer exist tomorrow. What happened but once might as well not have happened at all. If we have only one life to live, we might as well not have lived at all.
Franz denounces his wife, his entire public life behind to spend the rest of his days with Sabina. On waking up the next day he finds out that Sabina has left him for she cannot be pinned to the burden of being with someone in both her public and private life. She had always chosen lightness. The writer captures the plight of Franz with: She had left a man because she felt like leaving him. Had he persecuted her? Had he tried to take revenge on her? No. Her drama was a drama not of heaviness. What fell to her lot was not the burden but the unbearable lightness of being.
One day when Tomas returns from work he finds that Tereza has left for Prague, leaving him behind in Zurich all alone: For seven years he had lived bound to her, his every step subject to her scrutiny. She might as well have chained iron balls to his ankles. Suddenly his step was much lighter. He soared. He was enjoying the sweet lightness of being.

We understand things from where we come from, probably that is what the writer was trying to portray; that we see the world based on our choice for weight or lightness. Sabina chooses lightness, and Franz chooses the weight. Now to say that the characters of the novel made these choices on their own may not be correct.

It is that crossed border (the border beyond which my own "I" ends) which attracts me most. For beyond that border begins the secret the novel asks about. The novel is not the author's confession; it is an investigation of human life in the trap the world has become.
Light and Darkness
📔
Living for Sabina meant seeing. Seeing is limited by two borders: strong light, which blinds, and total darkness. Perhaps that was what motivated Sabina's distaste for all extremism. Extremes mean borders beyond which life ends, and a passion for extremism, in art and in politics, is veiled longing for death. Darkness attracted Franz as much as light. Darkness was pure, perfect, thoughtless, visionless; the darkness was without end, without borders; the darkness was the infinite we carry within us. (Yes, if you're looking for infinity, just close your eyes!) But for Sabina, darkness did not mean infinity; for her, it meant disagreement with what she saw, the negation of what was seen, the refusal to see.
Living in Truth
📔
What does it mean to live in truth? Putting it negatively is easy enough: it means not lying, not hiding, and not dissimulating. Sabina despised literature in which people give away all kinds of intimate secrets about themselves and their friends. A man who loses his privacy loses everything. And a man who gives it up of his own free will is a monster. That is why Sabina did not suffer in the least form having to keep her love secret. On the contrary, only by doing so could she live in truth. For Franz, living in truth meant breaking down the barrier between the private and the public. He was certain that the division of life into private and public spheres is the source of all lies.
Perhaps if they had stayed together longer, Sabina and Franz would have begun to understand the words they used. Gradually, timorously, their vocabularies would have come together, like bashful lovers, and the music of one would have begun to intersect with the music of other. But it was too late now.
In the part called Grand March, the writer introduces us to Kitsch. A German word that today in English means something of a poor taste because of excessive garishness or sentimentality. The writer defines the term a little differently: Kitsch is the absolute denial of shit, in both the literal and the figurative senses of the word; kitsch excludes everything from its purview which is essentially unacceptable in human existence.
The writer begins by attacking the literal shit: The daily defecation session is daily proof of the unacceptability of Creation. Either/or: either shit is acceptable (in which case don't lock yourself in the bathroom!) or we are created in an unacceptable manner. And then swiftly moves to the figurative shit: Kitsch is the aesthetic ideal of all politicians and all political parties and movements. This is one of his scintillating qualities, to discuss an idea in one dimension, and then apply it over a story stretching on geography and timeframes. At the beginning of the part, you'd wonder why is he talking about it, but by the time you reach the end of the part you'd bite your nails in astonishment.
With Kitsch, Kundera mocks Soviet society. Soviet society has made such progress that the basic conflict was no longer between good and evil but between good and better. The greatest conflict that could occur between two Russians was a lovers' misunderstanding; he thought she no longer loved her. But in the final scene, they would fall into each other's arms, tears of happiness trickling down their cheeks.
