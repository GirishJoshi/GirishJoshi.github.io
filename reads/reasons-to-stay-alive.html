Review
When I began reading this book I felt that this book is just going to make me feel depressed, it was painful at time to read what Matt goes through inside his head. And often I would ask, but why should I cling to it? Why should I finish reading it? I knew the answer, it was partly because of the pleasure of finishing things, and partly because of the hope that by the end of the book maybe everything in his life would be okay. But it's not a fairy tale, life is not a fairy tale. 'Monsters are real,' Stephen King said. 'And ghosts are real too. They live inside us, and sometimes, they win.' If I have to see the silver lining in what Stephen King said, then it would be that sometimes, they lose as well, and that's how life goes on for someone who is chronically depressed. And that was this book was about, that bouts of dark days would come and go, there will be storm inside your head on some days when the only thing that you can think about it that storm, but some days it will be sunshine. And it will be a beautiful sunshine.

When you come out of the storm you won't be the same person who walked in. That's what this storm's all about.' — Haruki Murakami, Kafka on the Shore
Neil Gaiman once said that writing is like walking naked on the street, and if you read this book, you'd see Matt naked, broken, vulnerable, and at his lows when he just wanted to end his life. But that's the thing about human will to survive, in this book, you also see him healing, holding, and learning to live with his illness anyway.
The chapters of this book are like journals of a boy on a journey to self-healing. They are messages that he is writing to himself but they speak to a volume of people around the globe. Matt spoke to my heart when he spoke about finding comfort in life, finding reason to stay alive in the books waiting to be read, and words waiting to be written.
